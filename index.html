Welcome the students. Let them know this module transitions from probability to inference. Today's focus is on estimating true population parameters and understanding what the margin of error actually tells us.
What is a Confidence Interval?
  • We have a sample proportion, which we write as $\hat{p}$.
  • When we estimate the standard deviation of a sampling distribution, we call it a standard error.
  • For a sample proportion, $\hat{p}$, the standard error is estimated by:
    $$SE(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}\hat{q}}{n}}$$
  • A confidence interval is an interval of values usually of the form: estimate ± margin of error.
Remind the class that the standard error is our estimated standard deviation of the sampling distribution. It tells us how much we expect sample proportions to vary from sample to sample.
Effect of Sample Size on Standard Error

Notice how the standard error decreases as the sample size ($n$) increases. (This curve assumes $\hat{p} = 0.5$ for the maximum possible variance).

Because $n$ is in the denominator of the square root, we must quadruple the sample size just to cut the error in half!

Point out the inverse square root relationship here. Emphasize that to cut the error in half, they need to quadruple the sample size—a classic conceptual check for exams.
Visualizing Confidence Intervals

Simulate drawing samples. Most intervals will capture the true population proportion (the vertical line), but a few will be "duds" that miss entirely!

Use this simulation to show what "95% confidence" actually means. It's about the reliability of the method across many samples, not the probability that a single, already-calculated interval contains the parameter.
Example 1: Facebook Status Updates
The Problem:

Late in 2010, Pew Research surveyed U.S. residents to ask about their use of social networking sites. Of the 156 respondents aged 18 to 22 who use Facebook, 48 said that they update their status at least daily. What can a study like this tell us about 18- to 22-year-old Facebook users in general? Find the 95% confidence interval.
A highly relatable context for the students. Before clicking to the solution, ask the class to identify the point estimate (p-hat) and the z-star value needed for a 95% confidence interval.
Solution 1: Facebook Status Updates
Given Values:

$n = 156$
$x = 48$
Confidence: 95%
  • Sample proportion, $\hat{p}$, of 48/156 or 30.8%.
  • Calculate the standard error:
    $$SE(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{(0.308)(0.692)}{156}} \approx 0.037$$
  • Reaching out 1.96 SEs on either side of $\hat{p}$ makes us 95% confident that we'll trap the true proportion, p.
  • $ME=1.96\times SE=1.96\times 0.037\approx 0.073$
  • We are 95% confident that between 23.4% and 38.2% of Facebook users between the ages of 18 and 22 update their status at least daily.
When discussing the conclusion, emphasize the need to state it in context. Translating the raw mathematical interval back into a meaningful real-world statement builds the kind of mathematical literacy emphasized in PISA frameworks.
Visualizing the 95% Interval

The sampling distribution model for $\hat{p}$ is Normal with a mean of p and a standard deviation we estimate to be 0.037.

Walk through the normal curve. The shaded area represents our 95% confidence level, reaching roughly two standard errors (1.96 precisely) on either side of the point estimate.
Margin of Error (ME)
  • The extent of the interval on either side of $\hat{p}$ is called the margin of error (ME).
  • The more confident we want to be, the larger the margin of error must be.
  • Every confidence interval is a balance between certainty and precision.
  • To change the confidence level, we change the critical value, $z^*$.
    $$ME = z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}\hat{q}}{n}}$$
Highlight the trade-off between certainty and precision. If we want to be 99% confident, our interval has to be much wider (less precise).
Example 2: Climate Change Poll
The Problem:

In April 2011, a Yale/George Mason poll of 1010 U.S. adults found that 40% of the respondents believed that scientists disagreed about whether global warming exists. Using the critical value of z and the standard error based on the observed proportion, what would be the margin of error for a 90% confidence interval?
Have the class identify how the 90% confidence level changes the critical value compared to the previous example. What is z-star for 90%? (1.645).
Solution 2: Climate Change Poll
Given Values:

$n = 1010$
$\hat{p} = 0.40$
Confidence: 90%
  • With $n = 1010$ and $\hat{p} = 0.40$:
    $$SE(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{(0.40)(0.60)}{1010}} \approx 0.0154$$
  • For a 90% confidence level, $z^* = 1.645$.
  • $ME = 1.645(0.0154) \approx 0.025$.
  • Now the margin of error is only about 2%, producing a narrower interval.
Notice how the margin of error is tighter here (around 2.5%). Ask the students to evaluate the trade-off of using a 90% interval instead of a 95% interval in a real-world polling context.
Assumptions and Conditions

To use a Normal model for the sampling distribution, check these conditions:

  • Independence / Randomization: Were the data sampled at random or generated from a properly randomized experiment?
  • 10% Condition: If you sample more than 10% of a population, the formula for the standard error won't be quite right.
  • Success/Failure Condition: We must expect at least 10 "successes" ($n\hat{p}\geq 10$) and at least 10 "failures" ($n\hat{q}\geq 10$).
Stress that these conditions must be explicitly checked before just plugging numbers into the formula. Showing that the model is appropriate is just as important as the math itself.
Example 3: The Death Penalty
The Problem:

In October 2010, the Gallup Poll asked 510 randomly sampled adults if they believe the death penalty is applied fairly. 58% answered "Fairly". What can we conclude about the opinions of all adults? Build a 95% confidence interval.
Let them verify the Success/Failure condition on their own first. 58% of 510 is about 296 successes, well over 10.
Solution 3: The Death Penalty
Given Values:

$n = 510$
$\hat{p} = 0.58$
Confidence: 95%
  • Check Conditions: Random sample, <10% of pop, $510(0.58) \ge 10$.
  • Find standard error:
    $$SE(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{(0.58)(0.42)}{510}} \approx 0.022$$
  • For 95% confidence, $z^* = 1.96$. $ME = 1.96(0.022) = 0.043$.
  • Conclusion: I am 95% confident that between 53.7% and 62.3% of all U.S. adults think that the death penalty is applied fairly.
Review the standard error calculation. Make sure they understand why we use p-hat when the true population parameter p is unknown.
Choosing Your Sample Size
  • The question of how large a sample to take is an important step in planning any study.
  • To find $n$, we need a value for $\hat{p}$. If we don't know it, we use $\hat{p} = 0.5$ because it determines the largest sample necessary regardless of the true proportion.
  • We solve for $n$ using the margin of error formula:
    $$ME = z^*\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}\hat{q}}{n}}$$
  • This gives us the formula for $n$:
    $$n=\frac{z^{*2}\hat{p}\hat{q}}{ME^2}$$
  • When computing sample sizes, we always round up to the next whole number.
Explain why we use 0.5 for p-hat when it's unknown—it maximizes the numerator product (0.25), ensuring the most conservative (largest) sample size estimate. Remind them to ALWAYS round up to ensure they hit the required ME!
Example 4: Credit Card Marketing
The Problem:

A credit card company is about to send out a mailing to test the market for a new credit card. A pilot study suggests that about 0.5% of the people receiving the offer will accept it. To be within a tenth of a percentage point (0.001) of the true rate with 95% confidence, how big does the test mailing have to be?
This requires algebraic manipulation of the margin of error formula. Have them write down the formula and attempt to isolate n before showing the solution.
Solution 4: Credit Card Marketing
Given Values:

$\hat{p} = 0.005$
$ME = 0.001$
Confidence: 95% ($z^* = 1.96$)
  • Using the estimate $\hat{p} = 0.5\%$:
    $$0.001 = 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{(0.005)(0.995)}{n}}$$
  • Squaring both sides and solving for $n$:
    $$n = \frac{1.96^2(0.005)(0.995)}{(0.001)^2}$$
  • $n \approx 19,111.96$.
  • Rounding up, we need a sample size of 19,112.
Walk through the algebra carefully. The rounding rule is crucial here; even if the answer was 19,111.01, we would still round up to 19,112 to guarantee the margin of error is strictly met.
Exercise 1: Lying About Age
The Problem:

Pew Research polled a random sample of 799 U.S. teens. 49% reported they had misrepresented their age online.

a) What is the standard error?
b) Find the 95% confidence interval.
Have the students work this out in groups or individually as a quick formative assessment.
Solution 1: Lying About Age
Given Values:

$n = 799$
$\hat{p} = 0.49$
Confidence: 95%
  • a) Standard Error Calculation:
    $$SE(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{0.49 \times 0.51}{799}} \approx 0.018$$
  • b) Margin of Error ($ME$) Calculation:
    $ME=1.96\times SE=1.96\times 0.018\approx 0.035$
  • c)Confidence Interval Calculation ($\hat p\pm ME$):
    $\hat p-ME=0.49 - 0.035\approx 0.455$
    $\hat p-ME=0.49 + 0.035\approx 0.525$
  • d) We are 95% confident that, if we were to ask all U.S. teens whether they have misrepresented their age online, between 45.5% and 52.5% of them would say they have.
Check their interpretations for part b. Make sure they use language indicating confidence about the *population* proportion, not just the sample.
Exercise 5: Wrong Direction
The Problem:

A survey asked a random sample of 402 adult Wisconsin residents whether they think things in the country are going in the right direction or in the wrong direction. 66% said that things were going in the wrong direction.

a) Calculate the margin of error for 90% confidence.
b) Would the margin of error be larger or smaller for 95% confidence?
Part b is a great conceptual check. Ask for a show of hands before revealing the answer.
Solution 5: Wrong Direction
Given Values:

$n = 402$
$\hat{p} = 0.66$
Confidence: 90%
  • a) $SE = \sqrt{\frac{0.66 \times 0.34}{402}} \approx 0.0236$. The Margin of Error using $z^* = 1.645$ is $1.645 \times 0.0236 \approx 0.039$, or 3.9%.
  • b) The ME for 95% confidence would be larger. The critical value of 1.96 is greater than the 1.645 needed for 90% confidence.
Reiterate the relationship between confidence level and margin of error to close out the lesson. Higher confidence means a wider net (larger margin of error).
Seatwork Exercises

Please answer the following three problems on your own:

1. Conceptual Factors: Several factors are involved in the creation of a confidence interval. Among them are the sample size, the level of confidence, and the margin of error. Which statements are true?
a) For a given sample size, higher confidence means a smaller margin of error.
b) For a specified confidence level, larger samples provide smaller margins of error.
c) For a fixed margin of error, larger samples provide greater confidence.
d) For a given confidence level, halving the margin of error requires a sample twice as large.
2. Teenage Drivers: An insurance company checks police records on 582 accidents selected at random and notes that teenagers were at the wheel in 91 of them.
a) Create a 95% confidence interval for the percentage of all auto accidents that involve teenage drivers.
b) Explain what your interval means.
c) Explain what "95% confidence" means.
d) A politician says, "In one of every five auto accidents, a teenager is behind the wheel." Does your confidence interval support or contradict this statement? Explain.
3. Deer Ticks: Wildlife biologists inspect 153 deer taken by hunters and find 32 of them carrying ticks that test positive for Lyme disease.
a) Create a 90% confidence interval for the percentage of deer that may carry such ticks.
b) If the scientists want to cut the margin of error in half, how many deer must they inspect?
c) What concerns do you have about this sample?
Give the students sufficient time to work through these problems. Remind them to check assumptions and conditions carefully for the computational problems!
Seatwork 1: Solutions
1. Conceptual Factors:
  • a) False. Higher confidence requires a wider interval, meaning a larger margin of error.
  • b) True. Dividing by a larger $\sqrt{n}$ shrinks the standard error and the margin of error.
  • c) True. A larger sample gives more precision, allowing you to cover the same fixed margin of error with a higher level of confidence.
  • d) False. Because $n$ is under a square root, halving the margin of error requires a sample four times as large (not twice).
Seatwork 2: Solutions
Given Values:

$n = 582$
$x = 91$
$\hat{p} = \frac{91}{582} \approx 15.6\%$
Confidence: 95%
  • a) $SE(\hat{p}) = \sqrt{\frac{0.156 \times 0.844}{582}} \approx 0.015$. The $ME = 1.96(0.015) \approx 0.029$. The interval is (12.7%, 18.6%).
  • b) We are 95% confident, based on this sample, that the true proportion of all auto accidents involving teenage drivers is between 12.7% and 18.6%.
  • c) About 95% of all random samples of this size will produce confidence intervals that successfully contain the true population proportion.
  • d) Contradicts. One in five is 20%. The entire confidence interval (12.7% to 18.6%) is completely below 20%.
Seatwork 3: Solutions
Given Values:

$n = 153$
$x = 32$
$\hat{p} = \frac{32}{153} \approx 20.9\%$
Confidence: 90%
  • a) For 90%, $z^* = 1.645$.
    $SE = \sqrt{\frac{0.209 \times 0.791}{153}} \approx 0.033$.
    $ME = 1.645(0.033) \approx 0.054$.
    The interval is (15.5%, 26.3%).
  • b) To cut the margin of error in half, the sample size must be quadrupled. $153 \times 4 =$ 612 deer.
  • c) The sample may not be a true random representation of all deer. Legally hunted deer might not represent all locations, ages, or sexes equally (e.g., hunters often target older males).
